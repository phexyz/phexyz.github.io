Seven months ago, I quit working at a blockchain startup and started my own company. I met Steve and decided that with my own financial background and jack-of-all-trades skills in AI and software engineering, it was time to build a modern solution to commercial real estate's age-old data problem. During these months, I thoroughly enjoyed the freedom given by this new business. Because the well-being of the company is tied directly to my personal well-being, I began to have this vision in my head: building this company is like a pilgrimage, a spiritual practice rather than an engineering project. As the company grows and evolves, so do Steve and I. As we as founders evolve, so does the company. I have always thought of myself as an inventor or innovator. I want to build something new. The longer I build, the more I sense that building something might not be the end goal. What's really the end goal of pouring the majority of my time and energy of my youth? Why do people run marathons? The completion of the course, and in my case, finishing the product, is part of it. But unlike a marathon that has a finite length, building software is a never-ending course of new features, refactoring, and debugging. Every day we come up with new ideas of how our product fits into the wider market and new solutions to the specific data problem that we are trying to solve. The idea of "finishing" is much harder to grasp than running a marathon. This is a feature. The lack of "finishing" makes the journey of building much more salient. It's a pilgrimage, or 修行.

The tao that can be told
is not the eternal Tao
The name that can be named
is not the eternal Name.

Through the numerous discussions I have with Steve, I discover a truth about human cognition. We reason through language and names. What is a transaction? What is a property? What is a deal? These are constructs that we define, and in a rapidly changing startup, the meanings of these words can change every day. In our daily lives, we use names and language to communicate, and different parts of reality are communicated to others who have not experienced them using these linguistic constructs. In Laozi's eyes, these descriptions of reality (the ten thousand things) are categorically different from reality itself. Therefore, they are not eternal. This passive view of language makes me wonder if Laozi would have rejected the idea that next-word prediction could lead to ASI.
