## Thesis

Slopify is buildable as an 18+ discovery league for tiny AI-built apps, not as a retail equity casino. An unknown builder ships into a shared safety shell; a public lottery gives some apps a fair season of airtime; players try them and make play-money forecasts; held-out users—not traders—produce the product evidence; real customers can buy real work; and VCs receive a factual, permissioned discovery feed. The spectacle says, “I found this ridiculous thing first.” The economic promise says, “A builder can turn genuine demand into serious customer contracts, and a skilled scout can turn a long-run record into paid research.” Neither promise says that a fantasy position becomes equity or that a prediction automatically pays cash.

This is a mechanism-design proposal, not a legal opinion. The launch sequence includes specialist securities, gaming, promotions, privacy, employment-agency, payments, consumer-reporting, advertising, IP, and product-safety counsel.

## Unit and legal structure: a decisive answer

The original unit does not survive.

A pump.fun-style app token that appreciates, converts into startup ownership, or pays when a VC invests is a security in economic substance. Recording it as a token does not change the securities-law analysis; the SEC's 2026 staff statement says the format and ownership-recording method do not change application of federal securities laws. [SEC, Statement on Tokenized Securities](https://www.sec.gov/newsroom/speeches-statements/corp-fin-statement-tokenized-securities-012826-statement-tokenized-securities)

Regulation Crowdfunding does not rescue the instant curve. Reg CF requires issuer disclosures and an SEC-registered intermediary, caps the amount an issuer may raise, and generally prevents resale for one year. That is structurally incompatible with a continuous buy/sell bonding curve. [SEC, Regulation Crowdfunding](https://www.sec.gov/resources-small-businesses/exempt-offerings/regulation-crowdfunding) Reg A can permit a broader public offering, but qualification, auditing, reporting, per-issuer economics, and regulated secondary-market infrastructure make it the wrong launch primitive for thousands of disposable slop apps. [SEC, Regulation A](https://www.sec.gov/resources-small-businesses/exempt-offerings/regulation)

Slopify therefore has four legally and mechanically separated objects:

1. **Signal credits.** Every verified adult gets the same free, nontransferable, nonredeemable fantasy bankroll each season. Credits cannot be purchased, cashed out, topped up, converted, pledged, or made scarce for money. A bounded-loss play-money market forecasts objective app milestones.
2. **Founder Flags and records.** A Flag is a scarce, nontransferable “I found it early” receipt. It carries no ownership or payment right. Verification always includes the holder's privacy-preserving lifetime opportunity and miss denominator, so one lucky screenshot cannot hide years of bad picks.
3. **Ordinary paid contracts.** Builders can receive fixed exhibition fees, paid auditions, production contracts, and customer pilots. Scouts can receive payment for opted-in research or causal distribution work. Cash pays for defined services; it does not pay for holding a forecast. Builders and scouts never pay Slopify to access the first earning path.
4. **A later startup financing.** If a graduate raises, the issuer and its counsel choose a separate registered or exempt offering through the appropriate intermediary. Slopify credits, Flags, badges, placement, and work history never convert, set valuation, confer allocation priority, or trigger a buyout.

The public graduation reveal is product-only. It cannot include an open SAFE, security type, raise amount, valuation, offering deck, subscription link, indication of interest, or investment call to action. Rule 506(b), the common private-placement exemption, prohibits general solicitation; unrestricted websites and broad communications can be solicitation, and a paid membership does not manufacture a pre-existing substantive investor relationship. [SEC, General Solicitation](https://www.sec.gov/resources-small-businesses/capital-raising-building-blocks/general-solicitation) Capital communication moves into a counsel-selected Rule 148, registered-intermediary, Rule 506(c), or genuine pre-existing-relationship Rule 506(b) lane.

The blunt concession is also the clean product decision: **an early player cannot passively cash out because a VC invests.** The chance to make meaningful money remains, but it comes from building something customers buy or demonstrating enough sustained skill to win paid work. An optional Slop Drop can be a separate, equal-odds, free-entry giveaway, but it produces no diligence signal and launches only where promotions counsel approves it.

## The storyboard

The cast:

- **Mira**, an unknown builder with a weird app called Ghost Kitchen—an AI tool that turns leftover ingredients into five-minute cooking games.
- **Jules**, a player who loves finding strange apps before everyone else.
- **Priya**, a scout who wants to prove she can distinguish a meme from a product.
- **Noor**, a food-delivery product lead with a real research and pilot budget.
- **Dana**, a VC product researcher looking for unusual founder/product evidence, not a Slopify investment recommendation.
- **Slopify**, the house, which earns from hosting, whole-season media, research workflow, and client SaaS—not participant losses or financings.

The canonical user story is:

> Ship → draw → play → hidden test → paid work → Proof → product conversation.

There is deliberately no **early buyer** in the final mechanism. The **early finder** is the replacement role: they can earn a verified reputation and compete for paid work, but they never buy an app-linked claim. Each scene below therefore tests the early-finder incentive where the original plan tested the early buyer.

### Scene 1 — Mint: Mira ships into the Garage

Mira imports Ghost Kitchen into Slopify's low-authority Garage. The shared login belongs to the shell, not to the app. The app runs on a unique hostile-content origin with default-deny outbound access, capability-scoped consent, pinned dependencies, a declared software/data/model bill of materials, and a visible “untrusted slop” boundary. Garage publication is free and immediate if the binary safety and provenance checks pass. It carries no promise of season traffic, audit, paid work, or VC attention.

Mira receives one free Draft ticket for one app family. Slopify publishes the eligible pool, commits its Merkle root, and uses a public randomness beacon to draw season seats uniformly without replacement. No category, follower count, sponsor preference, similarity feature, payment, bond, source deposit, or extra polish changes Mira's odds. At least 30% of every public Cup's measured seats, fresh-user minutes, programming, and discovery-review capacity belongs to the sponsor-blind Open Frontier. The whole Frontier is treated as ordinary league cost, priced and 110%-reserved before a public Cup launches.

If Mira is drawn, Slopify offers the same fixed, non-contingent exhibition fee it offers every selected builder. Only then does Mira decide whether to sign a narrow contract allowing Slopify to host, instrument, and preserve the submitted sealed snapshot for the announced season and audit window. She can decline with no penalty; the beacon advances to the next alternate. Slopify gets evidence continuity for the season, not ownership of her startup.

Alignment in this scene:

- **Builder:** Mira pays nothing for eligibility, keeps her IP, and receives the same random shot as a known founder.
- **Early finder (original early-buyer role):** Jules sees a genuinely open supply pool instead of apps that bought placement.
- **Player:** the shell limits what an unknown app can do with identity, data, money, and devices.
- **VC/product researcher:** Dana sees the complete eligible and selected denominators, not a sponsor-curated winner set.
- **House:** Slopify caps cost before promising traffic and cannot turn hopeful builders into the revenue source.

### Scene 2 — Curve: Jules plays a fantasy market

Ghost Kitchen opens a free-credit market over one coherent set of mutually exclusive outcomes: none, bronze-only, silver-only, or gold. The displayed cumulative probabilities all come from one bounded-loss logarithmic market scoring rule, one preregistered oracle, one app version, and one outcome window. The market does not open until the snapshot, fallbacks, keys, data rights, cohorts, and oracle are actually locked.

Jules must complete the app's declared experience task before forecasting. Ordinary Crowd Heat can be sprayed for fun, but it cannot mint an official early-finder claim. Jules has only a few Founder Flags. Placing one locks a meaningful share of the finite fantasy bankroll and records the app, probability, position in the sequence, and time.

The share card is designed to go viral without lying. It might say:

> Jules flagged Ghost Kitchen at 7%. Flag 2 of 5 this season. Lifetime: 38 Flags, 6 hits, 27 misses, 3 canceled, 2 pending.

The identity provider proves the lifetime denominator without revealing Jules's legal identity or unrelated app accounts. Jules may keep the record private, but a cropped or detached screenshot loses verified-finder status.

This public market is spectacle, not diligence. Forecast-aware accounts form the spectacle cohort. Their use, retention, invitations, and payments are labeled as show effects and excluded from Show Hit and Venture Proof.

Alignment in this scene:

- **Builder:** Mira gets fun, legible attention without selling equity or promising a cash exit.
- **Early finder (original early-buyer role):** Jules can earn durable status, but only with scarce conviction and a visible lifetime miss history.
- **Player:** play is free, bounded, and unmistakably noncash.
- **VC/product researcher:** Dana can watch the cultural signal while knowing it is contaminated by the show and excluded from product claims.
- **House:** Slopify gets pump.fun-like motion and shareability with bounded subsidy and without becoming the counterparty, custodian, exchange, or issuer.

### Scene 3 — Flywheel: the show creates attention; the experiment creates evidence

Every selected app receives a guaranteed randomized exploration floor. A capped remainder of traffic can adapt toward apps with lower-confidence-bound voluntary return, meaningful task completion, reliability, organic invitation acceptance, delayed benefit, low regret, and low abuse. Exploitation has diminishing returns and app/category ceilings. Heat, fantasy volume, sponsorship, and raw session length do not buy placement.

The crucial separation is invisible but load-bearing. Slopify recruits a cryptographically disjoint product cohort that cannot see the market, rank, receipt, sponsor narrative, comments, or season identity until follow-up closes. Confirmatory users are fresh to Slopify or have a long washout and evaluate only one focal app. Reused panels can screen cheaply, but they cannot confirm a product claim.

For products with network effects, Slopify uses cluster, saturation, or marketplace-cell experiments rather than pretending users are independent. For target-market fit, it recruits an external declared population. For willingness to pay, only uncompensated, real-channel economic commitment after discounts, refunds, CAC, cooling-off, and renewal counts. Panel-funded purchases never become demand.

Slopify reports three things separately:

- product behavior before show exposure;
- incremental lift caused by Slopify placement;
- the post-show ecosystem outcome.

Alignment in this scene:

- **Builder:** Mira is rewarded for a useful, reliable, low-regret product rather than merely a loud launch.
- **Early finder (original early-buyer role):** Jules's public story becomes more exciting if the pick survives a hidden test, but Jules cannot influence that test.
- **Player:** public play remains theatrical while quiet cohorts protect users from manipulative retention tricks.
- **VC/product researcher:** Dana gets causal and target-scoped evidence rather than a leaderboard mislabeled as traction.
- **House:** Slopify can sell trusted programming and a reproducible evidence terminal; attention fraud cannot directly increase revenue.

### Scene 4 — Anti-manipulation: Priya cannot grade her own homework

Identity and eligibility use pairwise credentials and season nullifiers. A single global privacy accountant governs every aggregate release, dashboard, notebook, API, and alert, so customers cannot reconstruct a small cohort by differencing separate exports. Slopify's clean-room enclave can examine device, referral, relationship, exposure, and payment graphs, but product teams, sponsors, and VCs receive scoped reason codes and sensitivity bands rather than the raw social graph.

Builder/affiliate accounts cannot scout their own app. Public-information Scout Quests assign Priya a common random slate and a hashed information packet with a cutoff. That credential measures judgment from a comparable information set. Priya's open-ended research remains a separate, self-authored portfolio; it is not blended into a universal score. A sensitive vulnerability goes through coordinated disclosure, earns a separate bounty and priority record, and cannot be used as an option to cancel a bad forecast.

Proper scoring rules reward honest probabilities only under their assumptions. Winner-take-all forecasting makes extreme or strategic reports attractive, so Slopify never pays the public according to rank. [Witkowski et al., “Incentive-Compatible Forecasting Competitions”](https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.2022.4410) Public forecasts yield status; paid research has its own client contract and deliverable.

The oracle denies Mira an upside-only withdrawal option. Once the market activates, builder-controlled withdrawal, credential withholding, unapproved mutation, support refusal, or dependency shutdown resolves to none if it prevents a higher state from being measured. Cancellation is reserved for enumerated exogenous platform-wide, legal, safety, or force-majeure events. Ambiguous control produces visible bounds and no positive finder credit until adjudication.

Alignment in this scene:

- **Builder:** honest Mira is not racing funded Sybils, rival sabotage, or insiders.
- **Early finder (original early-buyer role):** Jules and Priya keep credible records because misses, cancellations, restricted access, and lifetime opportunity counts cannot be silently erased.
- **Player:** raw identity and behavior graphs are not sold to every subscriber.
- **VC/product researcher:** Dana sees manipulation risk, uncertainty, lineage, exclusions, and audit disagreement rather than a magic “fraud-free” score.
- **House:** Slopify's long-run asset is the credibility of the record; fixed fees and independent outcome control reduce the gain from manufacturing a winner.

### Scene 5 — Graduation: Ghost Kitchen earns a scoped claim and real work

Evidence matures in four visible steps:

1. **Heat:** live entertainment attention only.
2. **Screen:** an early, sequential, unreplicated estimate.
3. **Signal:** fresh-cohort confirmation of the locked short-horizon claim.
4. **Proof:** mature, versioned evidence after the declared retention, welfare, economic, privacy, safety, and appeal windows.

Ghost Kitchen can earn:

- **Show Hit**, a scoped claim about usage, return, task benefit, low regret, reliability, and safety; and/or
- **Venture Proof**, which additionally requires target-market fit, real-channel economic commitment, credible acquisition lift, survival/renewal, and clean residual IP/control.

These are expiring claims about one hash, permission manifest, population, window, and evidence version—not certification of Mira, her company, future releases, investment quality, or clinical/financial validity. A changed app carries forward only unaffected claim components. Optional measurement is not declared statistically neutral: random capacity loss, structural missingness, and strategic nonapplication/withdrawal are labeled differently. Transparent pre-outcome cohorts, random invitations, full selection funnels, and partial-identification bounds show what can and cannot be inferred. [Manski, “Partial Identification with Missing Data”](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijar.2004.10.006)

Money enters through three honest doors:

- Mira is already paid the fixed exhibition fee.
- A blind staged Build-Off can give every shortlisted builder a paid audition, then offer a bounded market-rate production contract for accepted absolute milestones.
- Noor can directly buy a pilot that her company actually values. A six-figure headline must be a visible stack of independent, venture-safe customer contracts—not one Slopify grant multiplied into pretend traction.

Mira pays no bond, hosting fee, audit fee, clearance fee, or training fee to access those doors. Any post-contract retainage comes only from the accepted contract price and cannot claw back accepted work. This also avoids turning “we help you make money and find customers” into a pay-to-enter business opportunity; the FTC says required payment plus certain customer/account assistance claims can bring an arrangement within the Business Opportunity Rule. [FTC, Business Opportunity Rule guidance](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/selling-work-home-or-other-business-opportunity-revised-rule-may-apply-you-1)

Priya can seek paid research work through one of two jurisdiction-specific lanes. A licensed employer-fee-paid agency partner owns matching where local law reaches paid procurement of “employment or engagements.” Elsewhere Slopify is a genuinely passive board with applicant-initiated contact and no shortlist, rank, recommendation, negotiation, or placement claim. [New York General Business Law §171](https://www.nysenate.gov/legislation/laws/GBS/171) Payments are either cash-escrowed or backed by a licensed factor/insurer's underwritten, nonrecourse enterprise payment guarantee, allowing credible Net-30/60 buyers without making scouts finance them.

Dana's Discovery Membership guarantees a human product review, response SLA, structured feedback, and rotating office hours—not investment interest. Organic watchlisting, follow-up questions, second meetings, team review, and founder-authorized direct contact are reported separately after a cooling period. If those voluntary actions remain rare, Slopify must sell “feedback,” not “VC discovery.”

Alignment in this scene:

- **Builder:** Mira earns before financing, retains core IP, can choose Studio or Venture terms, and receives a real product conversation rather than a fake investment promise.
- **Early finder (original early-buyer role)/scout:** Jules keeps status; Priya can compete for payment-protected work without buying access or receiving a securities-finder fee.
- **Player:** no one converts play behavior into a cap-table instrument.
- **VC/product researcher:** Dana receives scoped evidence, uncertainty, and a direct founder-controlled product channel, while making her own investment judgment later.
- **House:** Slopify earns fixed media, hosting, evidence/workflow, and client SaaS revenue—not carry, participant loss, financing success fees, or inflated attention.

### Scene 6 — After: Mira can leave

At season end, the Draft's public exhibition license ends unless Mira separately accepts a compensated production/continuity contract. Slopify preserves the audit artifact and historical record; it does not own Ghost Kitchen, Mira's brand, customers, roadmap, or future versions.

Before measured status, every app implements a user-initiated exit bridge. A player can export their own app-specific data, settings, receipts, and paid entitlements in an open signed package. If Mira supplies a verified destination, the player can reconsent to link that one account. Mira never receives a bulk identity or contact dump. The trust standard, verification keys, evidence, and badge history are portable across accredited certifiers and survive Slopify's failure in offline-verifiable form.

If Mira later raises a SAFE or priced round, it happens through the separate issuer/intermediary lane. Jules and Priya own nothing unless they independently invest under that offering's rules. The permanent “found at” record still compounds reputationally, and Mira's historical evidence remains available with its date, version, scope, later failures, and departure status.

Alignment in this scene:

- **Builder:** Mira's credible exit option prevents platform hold-up; staying is attractive only when ongoing services are worth buying.
- **Early finder (original early-buyer role)/scout:** the discovery record survives even if the app or Slopify moves, but it never mutates into fictional equity.
- **Player:** portability requires individual consent and does not make identity a hostage.
- **VC/product researcher:** Dana can diligence an ordinary company with a reproducible historical artifact and then use ordinary financing rights.
- **House:** Slopify retains its honest asset—the evidence and discovery history—while recurring revenue must come from continuing usefulness.

## The backstage control stack

The storyboard stays simple because the complexity sits behind it:

- **Product authority:** unique origins, capability tokens, sealed builds, full client/server/model/prompt/data/human-operations manifests, default-deny egress, prompt-injection information-flow controls, accessible exit, and category-specific safety gates.
- **Scope exclusions at launch:** no youth/mixed-audience, clinical decision, personalized financial/legal, consequential employment/housing/credit/insurance, or uncontrolled public UGC/agent authority. Those require separate regulated lanes, not disclaimers.
- **Measurement integrity:** fresh held-out cohorts, preregistered claims, multiplicity budgets across app families and pivots, version lineage, network-aware experiments, external target panels, randomized placement holdouts, welfare hard gates, and independent shadow audits.
- **Evidence integrity:** signed short-lived claim cards, automatic supersession, material-change impact graphs, equal-prominence founder response, portable standards, multiple certifiers, transparent selection-correlated missingness, and no badge-absence ranking.
- **Work integrity:** no speculative custom work; random paid shortlist among equally qualified overflow; paid auditions before custom tests; client-funded or guaranteed payment; builder-controlled counsel benefit; objective contract-rights matrix; no pay-to-earn.
- **Governance:** one published decision-rights constitution. Safety can pause; auditors control evidence validity; the PSP controls safeguarded money; the standards trust controls claim rendering; and an insured Season Steward coordinates incidents through precommitted states, clocks, random alternates, reserves, and automatic defaults. The house cannot override a bad result.
- **Systemic risk:** independently keyed release cells, concentration limits, catastrophe stress capital, run-off insurance review, vendor failover/export drills, offline-verifiable records, a public recovery waterfall, and a willingness to stop growth when capital binds.

## Residual risks that survived all 100 rounds

1. **The hook is weaker than the original casino.** “Spot it and automatically get rich” is more viral than “build a long, auditable record and compete for work.” Honest separation may make the product less culturally explosive.
2. **Fresh evidence is expensive.** New users, long follow-up, moderation, auditors, privacy, appeals, insurance, and sponsor-blind Frontier capacity are real COGS. A lightweight media business may not support an evidence business.
3. **VC utility may not monetize.** Funds may value proprietary access and allocation more than a neutral terminal. Slopify deliberately refuses both. Guaranteed feedback can exist while genuine voluntary founder follow-through remains low.
4. **Manipulation remains possible.** Real-person proxy rings, paid households, patient collusion, off-platform promotion, leaked packets, adaptive maintenance, and hidden related-party customer payments cannot be eliminated without destroying privacy and openness.
5. **Evidence is selected and local.** Optional participation is informative; target panels can still be wrong; rare winners can fail replication; network effects can be unidentified; and short-horizon app behavior does not prove durable company execution.
6. **The badge can still become a gate.** Slopify can prohibit its own absence filters and accept equivalent evidence, but it cannot stop off-platform buyers from treating silence, expiry, or withdrawal as bad news.
7. **Founder flight is reduced, not solved.** The sealed snapshot preserves a season and historical evidence. It cannot preserve founder taste, future iteration, community, a disappearing third-party API, or a live company.
8. **Legal classification is functional and fragmented.** Employment-agency, worker-classification, consumer-reporting, payment, promotions, privacy, advertising, tax, IP, and state laws can attach despite carefully separated labels. Partners can deplatform the product.
9. **Governance can fail.** An independent trust, auditor, insurer, or Season Steward can be captured, slow, insolvent, or wrong. A constitutional state machine reduces bargaining but creates key-person and boundary risk.
10. **Safety tails remain systemic.** A shared login and app shell concentrate trust. Isolation, reserves, insurance, and failover reduce blast radius; they do not make an open AI-app platform safe.

## Blunt verdict

**Buildable: yes, as a narrow media + product-research + procurement league. Buildable as the original retail “buy app equity on a curve and cash out when a VC arrives” product: no.**

The minimum honest launch is not a public casino. It is:

1. a free low-authority Garage that measures app supply and hosting/moderation cost;
2. one private, anchor-customer-funded Founding Challenge with a handful of paid auditions;
3. a small public Cup only after the Open Frontier, fresh cohorts, claims, refunds, and catastrophe reserve are fully funded; and
4. expansion only after customers renew and voluntary product follow-up proves that “VC discovery” means more than mandatory review.

The kill metrics are concrete: eligible apps per season; cost per safe measured app; fresh-user acquisition and follow-up cost; unaided comprehension of credits versus cash; builder paid-shortlist conversion; actual builder/scout net earnings and zero-earner share; client renewal; evidence correction/false-signal rate; organic second meetings and team review; incident/appeal cost; and consolidated contribution margin after the sponsor-blind Frontier.

The single riskiest bet is **not law, Sybils, or builder flight; those can be bounded by narrowing the product. It is whether the same event can be entertaining enough that people share it, rigorous enough that VCs trust it, and valuable enough that customers pay the full cost of producing independent evidence.** Virality wants speed, tribal heat, and simple winners. Diligence wants separation, slow follow-up, uncertainty, and visible failures. Slopify is viable only if the storyboard makes the first layer irresistible while the backstage system keeps the second layer uncontaminated.
